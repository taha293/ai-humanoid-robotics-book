# Glossary of Terms

This glossary provides definitions for key terms and concepts used throughout the Physical AI & Humanoid Robotics course.

## A

**Actuator**: A component of a robot that converts control signals into physical movement. Examples include servo motors, stepper motors, and hydraulic cylinders.

**AI (Artificial Intelligence)**: The simulation of human intelligence processes by machines, especially computer systems. In robotics, AI enables robots to perceive, reason, learn, and make decisions.

**Artificial Neural Network (ANN)**: A computing system inspired by biological neural networks that constitute animal brains. Used for pattern recognition and machine learning in robotics.

## B

**Behavior Tree**: A hierarchical structure used in robotics and game AI to organize and control the behavior of autonomous agents. It provides a more flexible alternative to finite state machines.

**Biological Motion**: Movement patterns that are characteristic of living organisms, often used as inspiration for robotic locomotion and manipulation.

**Body Schema**: A representation of the robot's own body structure and configuration, used for self-awareness and movement planning.

## C

**Cartesian Space**: The 3D space defined by X, Y, and Z coordinates, used to describe positions and orientations in the environment.

**Cognitive Architecture**: The structural organization of an intelligent agent, defining how different components (perception, memory, reasoning, action) are organized and interact.

**Collision Detection**: The computational problem of detecting the intersection of two or more objects, crucial for safe robot navigation.

**Computer Vision**: A field of artificial intelligence that trains computers to interpret and understand the visual world, enabling robots to process visual information.

**Control Theory**: An engineering and mathematics field that deals with the behavior of dynamical systems with inputs, and how their behavior is modified by feedback.

## D

**Deep Learning**: A subset of machine learning that uses neural networks with many layers to learn complex patterns from large datasets.

**Degrees of Freedom (DOF)**: The number of independent movements a mechanical system can make, corresponding to the number of joints or independent actuators in a robot.

**Dexterous Manipulation**: The skillful and precise handling of objects using robot hands or end-effectors, often involving multiple contact points.

**Domain Randomization**: A technique in simulation where environment parameters are randomized to improve the transfer of learned behaviors from simulation to reality.

## E

**Embodied AI**: Artificial intelligence that is integrated with a physical body that can interact with the real world, as opposed to purely virtual AI systems.

**Embodiment**: The concept that intelligence emerges from the interaction between an agent and its physical environment, emphasizing the role of the body in cognition.

**End-Effector**: The device at the end of a robotic arm designed to interact with the environment, such as a gripper or tool.

**Episodic Memory**: A type of memory system that stores specific events and experiences, used in robotics for learning from past interactions.

## F

**Forward Kinematics**: The use of joint parameters to compute the position and orientation of the end-effector of a robot arm.

**Force Control**: A control strategy that regulates the forces exerted by a robot on its environment, important for tasks requiring physical interaction.

**Fused Reality**: The integration of real and virtual elements to create enhanced or mixed reality experiences for human-robot interaction.

## G

**Gaussian Process**: A non-parametric Bayesian approach used for regression and classification, useful for modeling uncertainty in robotic systems.

**Generalization**: The ability of a robot or AI system to apply learned behaviors to new situations not encountered during training.

**Gripper**: A device at the end of a robot arm designed to grasp and manipulate objects.

## H

**Haptic Feedback**: The use of touch and motion feedback to communicate with the user, important for teleoperation and human-robot interaction.

**Heuristic**: A practical approach to problem-solving that may not be optimal but is sufficient for achieving immediate goals, often used in robot planning.

**Human-Robot Interaction (HRI)**: The field of study focusing on the design, development, and evaluation of robots for interaction with humans.

## I

**Inverse Kinematics**: The mathematical process of determining the joint parameters needed to place the end-effector of a robot arm in a specific position and orientation.

**Imitation Learning**: A machine learning technique where an agent learns to perform tasks by observing and mimicking expert demonstrations.

**Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU)**: A device that measures and reports a body's specific force, angular rate, and sometimes the magnetic field surrounding the body.

## J

**Joint Space**: The space defined by the joint angles of a robot, as opposed to Cartesian space which is defined by position coordinates.

## K

**Kinematics**: The branch of mechanics concerned with the motion of objects without reference to the forces that cause the motion.

**Knowledge Representation**: The field of artificial intelligence dedicated to representing information about the world in a form that a computer system can utilize to solve complex tasks.

## L

**LIDAR (Light Detection and Ranging)**: A remote sensing method that uses light in the form of a pulsed laser to measure distances, commonly used in robotics for mapping and navigation.

**Locomotion**: The ability to move from one place to another, a fundamental capability for mobile robots.

**Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)**: A type of recurrent neural network capable of learning order dependence in sequence prediction problems.

## M

**Manipulation**: The ability of a robot to physically interact with objects in its environment, typically using an arm and gripper.

**Markov Decision Process (MDP)**: A mathematical framework for modeling decision-making in situations where outcomes are partly random and partly under the control of a decision maker.

**Machine Learning**: A method of data analysis that automates analytical model building, enabling robots to improve their performance with experience.

**Motion Planning**: The computational problem of finding a sequence of valid configurations that moves an object from a source to a destination.

## N

**Natural Language Processing (NLP)**: A field of AI focused on the interaction between computers and humans through natural language, enabling voice interfaces for robots.

**Neural Network**: A computing system based on the structure and functions of biological neural networks, used for pattern recognition in robotics.

## O

**Obstacle Avoidance**: The capability of a robot to detect and navigate around obstacles in its path.

**Omnidirectional**: The ability to move in any direction, typically used to describe mobile robot platforms.

**Ontology**: A formal naming and definition of the types, properties, and interrelationships of entities for a particular domain.

## P

**Path Planning**: The process of determining a route for a robot to follow from a starting point to a goal while avoiding obstacles.

**Perception**: The process by which robots acquire and interpret sensory information about their environment.

**Physical AI**: A field that combines artificial intelligence with physical systems, emphasizing the interaction between intelligence and the physical world.

**Probabilistic Robotics**: An approach to robotics that explicitly accounts for uncertainty in sensing and actuation using probability theory.

**Proactive Behavior**: The ability of a robot to anticipate future events and act accordingly, rather than merely reacting to current situations.

## R

**Reinforcement Learning**: A type of machine learning where an agent learns to make decisions by performing actions and receiving rewards or penalties.

**Robot Operating System (ROS)**: A flexible framework for writing robot software, providing hardware abstraction, device drivers, and message passing.

**Reactive System**: A system that responds to environmental changes, typically with simple, direct mappings between sensors and actuators.

**Recurrent Neural Network (RNN)**: A class of artificial neural networks where connections between nodes form a directed graph along a temporal sequence, suitable for time-series data.

**RGB-D Camera**: A camera that captures both color (RGB) and depth (D) information, useful for 3D scene understanding.

## S

**Sensor Fusion**: The process of combining sensory data from multiple sources to achieve better accuracy and reliability than could be achieved by using a single sensor.

**SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping)**: The computational problem of constructing or updating a map of an unknown environment while simultaneously keeping track of an agent's location within it.

**Social Robotics**: The study of robots that interact with humans in a socially acceptable manner.

**State Estimation**: The process of determining the state of a system (position, velocity, etc.) from noisy sensor measurements.

**Supervised Learning**: A machine learning approach where the model is trained on labeled data, learning to map inputs to outputs.

## T

**Task Planning**: The process of decomposing high-level goals into sequences of executable actions for a robot.

**Teleoperation**: The remote operation of a robot, where a human operator controls the robot's actions from a distance.

**Trajectory**: A path that an object follows through space as a function of time, important for robot motion planning.

## V

**Vision-Language-Action (VLA)**: An integrated approach that combines visual perception, natural language understanding, and robotic action for human-robot interaction.

**Visual Servoing**: The control of robot motion using visual feedback, where the robot adjusts its movement based on visual information.

## W

**Whole-Body Control**: A control approach that coordinates all degrees of freedom of a robot simultaneously to achieve multiple tasks.

**Workspace**: The volume of space that a robot manipulator can reach with its end-effector.

## Z

**Zero Moment Point (ZMP)**: A concept used in robotics and biomechanics to propose a stability measure for legged robots, particularly important for humanoid balance.
